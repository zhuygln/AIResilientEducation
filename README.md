# AI-Resistant Education for Kids – Concept Document

## 1. Motivation and Mission

### Core motivations

- Protect and strengthen children’s **human** capacities (curiosity, empathy, agency, critical thinking) in an AI-saturated world.
- Intervene *before* harm or misuse, similar to good sex education or digital safety education: assume contact with AI is inevitable, and focus on prevention and preparation, not fear or bans.
- Offer families and schools a sane middle path between “ban all AI” and “let kids figure it out alone.”
- Teach kids to see AI as a powerful tool/“creature” they must learn to handle safely, not as a friend, a god, or a replacement for their own thinking.

### Mission Statement

**We help children become confident, resilient AI users—developing judgment, integrity, and human skills that no technology can replace.**

We teach kids and adults to see AI as a powerful tool to be used wisely, not a replacement for thinking, relationships, or responsibility.

---

## 2. Core Organizational Values

### 2.1 Child-centered dignity

- Children’s safety, privacy, emotional well-being, and long-term development come before convenience, profit, or hype.
- The organization treats every program as serving *children’s* interests first, not vendors or systems.

### 2.2 Human agency over automation

- AI should **augment**, not replace, human judgment, relationships, and responsibility.
- Kids learn that humans remain in charge; they are training the AI “dragon,” not obeying it.
- The org pushes back against uses of AI that remove meaningful human oversight.

### 2.3 Honesty and transparency

- Be explicit about:
  - When AI is used.
  - What AI can and cannot do.
  - Where its limits and risks are.
- Model and teach kids to be transparent: “I used AI to help with X,” instead of hiding or cheating.

### 2.4 Equity and inclusion

- Design materials that work across cultures and income levels.
- Avoid deepening digital divides or reinforcing bias.
- Teach children about fairness and how AI systems can inherit or amplify human bias.

### 2.5 Privacy and minimal data

- Collect and store as little child data as possible.
- Avoid sending identifiable student data to external AI services when possible.
- Prefer tools and practices that respect children’s privacy and do not profile or manipulate them.

### 2.6 Critical compassion

- Combine critical thinking (question, verify, resist manipulation) with compassion (understand effects of AI on people, especially vulnerable groups).
- Encourage kids to think about how AI impacts themselves, their friends, and society.

### 2.7 Evidence-informed practice

- Base curricula and policies on:
  - Child development research.
  - AI ethics and responsible AI frameworks.
  - Real classroom experience and feedback.
- Commit to revisiting and updating programs as evidence and technology change.

## 3. Guiding Principles for Programs and Partnerships

### 3.1 Human-first, AI-aware design

- Every program has explicit developmental goals (empathy, critical thinking, self-regulation). AI is context, not purpose.

### 3.2 Age-appropriate, staged learning

- Content evolves from concrete (grades 3–5) to complex (grades 6–8+). Themes repeat with increasing nuance: privacy, honesty, boundaries, identity.

### 3.3 Prevention over punishment

- We prepare kids *before* problems arise. When misuse happens, we focus on reflection and learning, not shame.

### 3.4 True partnership with adults

- We provide clear guidance, sample policies, ready-to-use lessons, and scripts. We help educators talk about AI as they would sex ed: early, honest, calm, value-based.

### 3.5 Vendor independence

- We maintain independence from specific AI companies. We disclose all partnerships and avoid endorsing tools for money or influence.

### 3.6 Walk the talk

- Our staff use AI transparently. We include youth voices in design. We review our own practices regularly to stay true to our values.

## 4. Conceptual Frame: AI as “Another Entity”

### 4.1 AI as a new kind of “other”

- Kids already learn different interaction rules for:
  - People (mutual respect, feelings, consent).
  - Animals (body language, care, boundaries).
- AI is “another entity”:
  - Human-like in language and style.
  - Not human in feelings, responsibility, or consciousness.
  - Requires its own interaction rules.

### 4.2 Desired social skills with AI

Kids should learn to:

- Recognize what AI is and is **not**:
  - “It can sound smart, but it doesn’t actually know or care like a person.”
- Follow “conversation rules” with AI:
  - What’s safe to ask.
  - What is unsafe (private info, intimate topics, self-harm, hatred).
  - How to stop and ask a human if something feels wrong.
- Manage emotions and attachment:
  - Name the feeling that AI seems like a friend.
  - Understand that the system does not have genuine care or responsibility.
  - Prefer human relationships for emotional and moral questions.

## 5. Metaphor: “Train Your AI Dragon”

### 5.1 Why this metaphor

- AI is like a dragon:
  - Powerful, surprising, helpful, and sometimes dangerous.
  - Follows training and patterns, not morals or feelings.
- Kids are the rider/trainer:
  - Learn commands, boundaries, and safety rules.
  - Know when NOT to bring the dragon into a situation (e.g., private, emotional, or integrity-sensitive tasks).
- Fits well with:
  - Children’s imagination.
  - The idea of discipline, responsibility, and growth.
  - The sex-ed analogy: you can’t pretend dragons don’t exist; you prepare kids to handle them.

### 5.2 Example lesson concepts (high-level)

Not full curriculum, just the framing:

- Lesson: “Meet your dragon”
  - What can it do?
  - What can’t it do?
  - How is it different from a friend or a pet?

- Lesson: “Safety rules for dragon riders”
  - 4–5 simple rules: no private info, double-check facts, ask adults, don’t use it to hurt others, don’t let it do your whole work.

- Lesson: “Dragons don’t have hearts”
  - AI doesn’t feel love, guilt, or responsibility.
  - Humans are responsible for actions and choices.

These sessions would be adapted by age band (3–5, 6–8 grades, etc.).

## 6. Alignment with Responsible AI Principles

The organization’s values should align with established responsible AI frameworks (e.g., OECD AI Principles, UNICEF AI for Children guidance, TeachAI, etc.).

### 6.1 Values ↔ Principles mapping (conceptual)

- **Child-centered dignity**  
  ↔ Child rights, safety, and well-being.

- **Human agency over automation**  
  ↔ Human oversight and agency (humans remain in control of important decisions).

- **Honesty and transparency**  
  ↔ Transparency and explainability (clear about AI’s role and limits).

- **Equity and inclusion**  
  ↔ Fairness, non-discrimination, inclusive access; avoid systems that deepen inequality.

- **Privacy and minimal data**  
  ↔ Data protection and privacy; minimize data collection and risk.

- **Evidence-informed practice**  
  ↔ Accountability and continuous improvement; review impact, adjust with new evidence.

### 6.2 Translating alignment into policies

Examples:

- Tool vetting:
  - No use of tools that profile or manipulate children, or that monetize child data in exploitative ways.
- Human oversight:
  - Avoid recommending systems that automate grading or discipline without real human review.
- Transparency:
  - In all materials and examples, clearly label where AI is used.
- Equity:
  - Always provide low-tech or no-tech versions of activities where possible.
- Privacy:
  - Provide simple privacy checklists to schools and parents; encourage minimal data practices.

### 6.3 Governance loop

- Create a small advisory group:
  - Educator, child psychologist, AI/ethics expert, youth voice.
- Give this group a specific remit:
  - Review new programs and partnerships against the values ↔ principles mapping.
- Schedule periodic reviews:
  - Once or twice a year, check whether programs still align with child-centered, responsible AI standards.

## 7. Target Age Groups and Interventions

### 7.1 Elementary school (approx. 8–11)

Focus:

- Feelings, safety, simple habits and rules, basic understanding of what AI is.
- Very concrete metaphors (dragons, robots, creatures).

Content themes:

- “What is AI?”
- “When should I ask a person instead of a machine?”
- “What is a secret? What is private?”
- “How do I know if something is true?”

### 7.2 Middle school (approx. 11–14)

Focus:

- Critical thinking, ethics, identity, social impact, and risk.
- More explicit link to:
  - Cheating and academic integrity.
  - Privacy and data traces.
  - Deepfakes, manipulation, and emotional attachment to chatbots.

Content themes:

- Bias and fairness.
- Relationship between AI and self-image.
- Transparent AI use in homework.
- Designing class norms for “healthy AI use.”
